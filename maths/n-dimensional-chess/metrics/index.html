Evaluating N-Dimensional Chess Pieces

published March 09, 2026

This page analyses the strength of the pieces defined previously. I will use three notions throughout.

  • Power: how many squares can be reached in one move.
  • Speed: how many moves are needed to travel between two squares.
  • Reach: whether every square of the board is accessible at all (for bishops).

Power is often position-dependent, so we may speak of best-case, worst-case, or average-case power: a rook can always move to exactly 7N7N7N squares, but a bishop varies enormously. This is also complicated by the geometry of high-dimensional cubes where almost all of the volume is concentrated near the boundary:

Since lower values for speed are preferable, this really represents a diameter: precisely, the diameter of the graph induced by this chess piece’s adjacency rules.

King

Power. From a position x\mathbf{x}x, the King threatens every square in {x+δ:∥δ∥∞=1}\{\mathbf{x} + \delta : \|\delta\|_\infty = 1\}{x+δ:∥δ∥∞​=1} that lies within the board. At a central square (any of the 6N6^N6N squares not touching any edge), each of the NNN components of δ\deltaδ ranges freely over {−1,0,+1}\{-1, 0, +1\}{−1,0,+1}, giving 3N−13^N - 13N−1 moves (with the −1-1−1 since the King cannot stay put): this is the best case. At each of the 2N2^N2N corners, each component has only 222 choices, giving 2N−12^N - 12N−1: this is the worst case.

In higher dimensions, note that lim⁡N→∞6N+2N8N=lim⁡N→∞(3/4)N+(1/4)N=0\lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{6^N + 2^N}{8^N} = \lim_{N \to \infty} (3/4)^N + (1/4)^N = 0limN→∞​8N6N+2N​=limN→∞​(3/4)N+(1/4)N=0, and so asymptotically no squares fall into these best or worst-case scenarios. More representative is the average case. Ignore the restriction on staying put for now. For a random square, each component of the displacement vector has three options unless the King is at the edge of the board along the corresponding axis. This yields three options in 6/86/86/8 of cases, and two options otherwise, for an average of 11/411/411/4 options for each component. These restrictions are independent, so there are (11/4)N(11/4)^N(11/4)N squares within a distance of 1. Subtracting the start square, we find that the King’s average-case power is (11/4)N−1(11/4)^N-1(11/4)N−1.

Knight

Power. Each Knight move activates exactly two of the NNN axes, displacing one by ±2\pm 2±2 and the other by ±1\pm 1±1. From a central square, we choose an axis which gets the 222 (NNN ways) and which gets the 111 (N−1N-1N−1 ways), and pick signs (444 ways), giving 4N(N−1)4N(N-1)4N(N−1) moves. From a corner, only one sign combination keeps both coordinates on the board, leaving N(N−1)N(N-1)N(N−1) moves.

For the average case, each potential move requires the ±2\pm 2±2 step to land on the board (probability 3/43/43/4) and the ±1\pm 1±1 step likewise (probability 7/87/87/8). Rook

Power. The Rook can always reach exactly 7N7N7N squares: along each of the NNN axes, there are 777 other values to slide to, regardless of the Rook’s current position. This makes the Rook unique among the pieces, as its power is completely position-independent with no distinction between best, worst, and average case.

This power grows linearly with NNN, in contrast to the King’s exponential 3N−13^N - 13N−1. In two dimensions, the Rook’s 141414 squares comfortably exceed the King’s best-case 888. But by N=3N = 3N=3 the King’s best case of 262626 has already overtaken the Rook’s 212121, and by N=10N = 10N=10 the comparison is absurd: 59,04859,04859,048 vs 707070.

For the average case, each coordinate independently agrees with probability 1/81/81/8 and differs with probability 7/87/87/8, so the distance follows a Bin⁡(N,7/8)\operatorname{Bin}(N, 7/8)Bin(N,7/8) distribution with mean 7N/87N/87N/8. Bishop

Reach. In two dimensions, the bishop is confined to squares of one colour. How does this constraint generalise? For the 222-bishop, the constraint is identical: each move preserves the parity of the coordinate sum ∑xi\sum x_i∑xi​ by changing two coordinates by ±k\pm k±k, altering the sum by the even (±k)+(±k)(\pm k) + (\pm k)(±k)+(±k). So the 222-bishop is confined to exactly half the board, via the same light/dark restriction as in two dimensions.

The NNN-bishop is even more constrained. Each move changes all NNN coordinates by ±k\pm k±k, so any two moves that differ in the sign of a single coordinate produce a net displacement of 2k2k2k in that coordinate and 000 in all others. This means the NNN-bishop can shift differences between individual coordinates, but only by even amounts. The reachable squares are those where every coordinate shares the same parity: either all coordinates are even or all are odd. On the 888-board, each parity class contains 4N4^N4N squares, so the NNN-bishop can reach 2⋅4N2 \cdot 4^N2⋅4N squares out of 8N8^N8N (a fraction 21−N2^{1-N}21−N of the board). For N=2N = 2N=2 this gives 1/21/21/2, but for N=10N = 10N=10 it is less than 0.2%0.2\%0.2%.

Conversely, the SSS-bishop breaks free entirely for N⩾3N \geqslant 3N⩾3.
