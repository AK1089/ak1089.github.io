N-Dimensional Chess

published March 01, 2026

I. Displacement Vectors

What is the nicest generalisation of each standard chess piece into an arbitrary NNN-dimensional board {0,…,7}N\{0, \ldots, 7\}^N{0,…,7}N? We seek a definition which is as natural and simple as possible and which matches the piece’s behaviour in two dimensions without privileging this case.

The most natural descriptions are in terms of displacement vectors: a piece may move from some space x\mathbf{x}x to another space x+δ\mathbf{x} + \deltax+δ if and only if the displacement vector δ\deltaδ lies within its move set (and, of course, if this lies within the board).

Write δ=(δ1,δ2,…,δN)\delta = (\delta_1, \delta_2, \dots, \delta_N)δ=(δ1​,δ2​,…,δN​) for the components of this displacement vector. For most pieces, we can decompose δ=kv\delta = k\mathbf{v}δ=kv where v∈{−1,0,+1}N∖{0}\mathbf{v} \in \{-1, 0, +1\}^N \setminus \{\mathbf{0}\}v∈{−1,0,+1}N∖{0} is a direction vector and k⩾1k \geqslant 1k⩾1 is an integer scalar. Non-sliding pieces (king, knight) fix k=1k = 1k=1; sliding pieces (rook, bishop, queen) allow any kkk. We classify directions by how many axes they involve: let S(v)=#{1⩽i⩽N:vi≠0}S(\mathbf{v}) = \#\{ 1 \leqslant i \leqslant N : v_i \neq 0\}S(v)=#{1⩽i⩽N:vi​=0} denote the number of active axes.

II. The Pieces

King

The king moves one step in any direction: horizontally, vertically, or diagonally. In terms of the displacement vector, every component satisfies ∣δi∣⩽1|\delta_i| \leqslant 1∣δi​∣⩽1, and at least one is nonzero. We can write this nicely in terms of the supremum norm: the condition is ∥δ∥∞=1\|\delta\|_\infty = 1∥δ∥∞​=1.

Knight

The knight also does not fit the sliding framework. Its displacement δ\deltaδ is a permutation of (±2,±1,0,…,0)(\pm 2, \pm 1, 0, \ldots, 0)(±2,±1,0,…,0): “move two units in some direction, then one unit in some other direction”. We can characterise this neatly with two norm conditions: ∥δ∥1=3\|\delta\|_1 = 3∥δ∥1​=3 and ∥δ∥∞=2\|\delta\|_\infty = 2∥δ∥∞​=2. Together these uniquely pick out the {2,1}\{2, 1\}{2,1} pattern, up to signs and axis choices.

Rook

The rook slides any distance along a single axis. Its direction vector has exactly one nonzero component, so S(v)=1S(\mathbf{v}) = 1S(v)=1: the displacement takes the form δ=kej\delta = k\mathbf{e}_jδ=kej​ for some basis vector ej\mathbf{e}_jej​ and nonzero integer kkk. We can also characterise this by ∥δ∥∞=∥δ∥1≠0\|\delta\|_\infty = \|\delta\|_1 \neq 0∥δ∥∞​=∥δ∥1​=0.

Bishop

In two dimensions, the bishop slides diagonally, moving along both axes simultaneously: S(v)=2S(\mathbf{v}) = 2S(v)=2. When we enter N>2N > 2N>2 dimensions, it is unclear whether to generalise this rule as S(v)=2S(\mathbf{v}) = 2S(v)=2 or S(v)=NS(\mathbf{v}) = NS(v)=N (or even 2⩽S(v)⩽N2 \leqslant S(\mathbf{v}) \leqslant N2⩽S(v)⩽N).

These three interpretations give rise to three types of bishop, which I shall term the 222-bishop, the NNN-bishop, and the SSS-bishop. In two dimensions, these are equivalent, but in higher dimensions this breaks down.

Queen

In two dimensions, the queen can slide any distance along a rank, file, or diagonal. There are two natural ways to phrase this:

  • Rook ∪\cup∪ Bishop: the queen can make any move that either a rook or a bishop could make.
  • Unrestricted-range King: the queen can move in any direction the king can, but for any distance k⩾1k \geqslant 1k⩾1 rather than just k=1k = 1k=1.

In two dimensions these coincide, but in higher dimensions we get three queen variants from combining the rook with our three bishop variants.

Pawn

I’m going to omit the pawn from this analysis: its movement is inherently stateful (having moved or not determines whether it can advance two squares), directional (its movement cannot be characterised by symmetric displacement constraints), and generally peculiar (it captures and advances differently, and en passant exists). All of these make pawns resist clean generalisation into higher dimensions.

III. Metrics

How powerful is a piece? In two-dimensional chess, the consensus ranks queen first, then the rook, followed by the bishop and knight (with the king omitted). In NNN dimensions, we need to be more precise about what “powerful” even means.

A natural starting point is power: how many squares can a piece reach in one move? This is position-dependent: a rook on an NNN-board can always reach exactly 7N7N7N squares from any starting position, but a bishop’s reach varies enormously (larger near the board’s centre and smaller near corners). We could resolve this using best-case power (maximised over all positions), worst-case power (minimised), or average-case power (averaged uniformly over all squares). Each has drawbacks: best-case power flatters pieces like the bishop which only achieve their peak in a narrow region, worst-case power penalises every piece equally for corner effects, and average-case power is complicated by the geometry of high-dimensional cubes, where almost all of the volume is concentrated near the boundary.

A second natural metric is speed: across all pairs of squares, how many moves does it take to travel between them? Again we can take the worst case or the average. Speed captures something different from power: a piece might threaten many squares locally but be slow to traverse the board, or vice versa. Since lower values are better, speed is really a diameter the length of the shortest path between two (randomly chosen or maximally distant) squares.

Finally, for bishops in particular, we should note that they have limited reach and cannot necessarily access all squares: in two dimensions, each bishop is confined to either the light or dark squares. How does this translate to higher dimensions?

Knight

Power. Each Knight move activates exactly two of the NNN axes, displacing one by ±2\pm 2±2 and the other by ±1\pm 1±1. Rook

Power. The Rook can always reach exactly 7N7N7N squares: along each of the NNN axes, there are 777 other values to slide to, regardless of the Rook’s current position. This makes the Rook unique among the pieces, as its power is completely position-independent with no distinction between best, worst, and average case.

This power grows linearly with NNN, in contrast to the King’s exponential 3N−13^N - 13N−1. In two dimensions, the Rook’s 141414 squares comfortably exceed the King’s best-case 888. Bishop

Reach. In two dimensions, the bishop is confined to squares of one colour. How does this constraint generalise?

The 222-bishop preserves the parity of the coordinate sum ∑xi\sum x_i∑xi​: each move changes two coordinates by ±k\pm k±k, altering the sum by (±k)+(±k)(\pm k) + (\pm k)(±k)+(±k), which is always even. So the 222-bishop is confined to exactly half the board — the same light-square / dark-square restriction as in two dimensions.

The NNN-bishop is more constrained. Each move changes all NNN coordinates by ±k\pm k±k, so any two moves that differ in the sign of a single coordinate produce a net displacement of 2k2k2k in that coordinate and 000 in all others. This means the NNN-bishop can shift individual coordinates, but only by even amounts. The reachable squares are those where every coordinate shares the same parity: either all coordinates are even or all are odd. On the 888-board, each parity class contains 4N4^N4N squares, so the NNN-bishop can reach 2⋅4N2 \cdot 4^N2⋅4N squares out of 8N8^N8N — a fraction 21−N2^{1-N}21−N of the board. For N=2N = 2N=2 this gives 1/21/21/2 (recovering the familiar result), but for N=10N = 10N=10 it is less than 0.2%0.2\%0.2%. The NNN-bishop becomes increasingly trapped in higher dimensions.

The SSS-bishop breaks free entirely for N⩾3N \geqslant 3N⩾3. It includes both S=3S = 3S=3 moves (say (1,1,1,0,…,0)(1, 1, 1, 0, \ldots, 0)(1,1,1,0,…,0)) and S=2S = 2S=2 moves (say (0,−1,−1,0,…,0)(0, -1, -1, 0, \ldots, 0)(0,−1,−1,0,…,0)). Composing these gives a net displacement of (1,0,0,0,…)(1, 0, 0, 0, \ldots)(1,0,0,0,…): effectively a single orthogonal move. Since it can simulate rook-like displacements along any axis, the SSS-bishop can reach every square on the board.
