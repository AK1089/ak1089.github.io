High-Dimensional Bishops are Fast

published March 09, 2026

As defined here under the Bishop section, the SSS-bishop moves by choosing two or more axes and moving along this diagonal (the same distance along each chosen axis). Although it is defined purely in terms of diagonal motion, for N⩾3N \geqslant 3N⩾3 it can access the entire board. This is easy to see via the pair of moves ei+ej+ek\mathbf{e}_i + \mathbf{e}_j + \mathbf{e}_kei​+ej​+ek​ followed by −ej−ek-\mathbf{e}_j-\mathbf{e}_k−ej​−ek​, which combine to form the unit displacement ei\mathbf{e}_iei​. More surprising than this reach is just how fast it can travel.

Theorem. For N⩾3N \geqslant 3N⩾3, the SSS-bishop can access any square from any starting square in at most three moves.

If you wish to see this in action, scroll to the bottom of the page.

In dimensions N⩾4N \geqslant 4N⩾4, we prove the stronger claim that we may access any square in precisely three moves, where also the lengths of these moves follow one of four very specific patterns: the length sequences (4,2,1)(4, 2, 1)(4,2,1), (4,1,3)(4, 1, 3)(4,1,3), and their reverses (1,2,4)(1, 2, 4)(1,2,4) and (3,1,4)(3, 1, 4)(3,1,4).

Proposition 1. For any pair of start and end squares on the N=4N = 4N=4 board, there exists a sequence of three valid moves from the start square to the end square whose lengths follow one of these four patterns.

Proof.

Fix a pattern (ℓ1,ℓ2,ℓ3)(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell_3)(ℓ1​,ℓ2​,ℓ3​). For a single coordinate, say from aaa to bbb, call a triple

(δ1,δ2,δ3)∈{0,±ℓ1}×{0,±ℓ2}×{0,±ℓ3} (\delta_1,\delta_2,\delta_3)\in\{0,\pm\ell_1\}\times\{0,\pm\ell_2\}\times\{0,\pm\ell_3\} (δ1​,δ2​,δ3​)∈{0,±ℓ1​}×{0,±ℓ2​}×{0,±ℓ3​}

admissible if the intermediate moves

a+δ1,a+δ1+δ2,a+δ1+δ2+δ3=b a+\delta_1,\qquad a+\delta_1+\delta_2,\qquad a+\delta_1+\delta_2+\delta_3=b a+δ1​,a+δ1​+δ2​,a+δ1​+δ2​+δ3​=b

all lie in {0,…,7}\{0,\dots,7\}{0,…,7}. Choosing such a triple independently for each of the four coordinates produces three displacement vectors

mj=(δ1,j,δ2,j,δ3,j,δ4,j)(j=1,2,3), \mathbf{m}_j=(\delta_{1,j},\delta_{2,j},\delta_{3,j},\delta_{4,j}) \qquad (j=1,2,3), mj​=(δ1,j​,δ2,j​,δ3,j​,δ4,j​)(j=1,2,3),

and conversely every three-move path of this pattern determines four such one-dimensional triples. Thus the whole problem factorises by coordinate, except for one global condition: each move must be a genuine bishop move, so it must be active on at least two coordinates.

To track this, for an admissible triple we record only its support mask

(1δ1≠0,1δ2≠0,1δ3≠0)∈{0,1}3. (\mathbf{1}_{\delta_1\neq 0},\mathbf{1}_{\delta_2\neq 0},\mathbf{1}_{\delta_3\neq 0})\in\{0,1\}^3. (1δ1​=0​,1δ2​=0​,1δ3​=0​)∈{0,1}3.

If each coordinate is admissible as above, a valid 444-dimensional path exists exactly when we may choose one support mask for each coordinate so that, in each of the three positions, at least two of the four masks contain a 111. No other interaction between the coordinates remains.

There are only 82=648^2=6482=64 possible ordered pairs (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) in one coordinate. Since the four coordinates may be permuted, it suffices to check multisets of four such coordinate-pairs rather than all 888^888 start/end pairs.

Having shown the argument for N=4N = 4N=4, we lift the valid move sequences to higher-dimensional boards.

Proposition 2. For N>4N > 4N>4, we may construct this sequence of three moves on the first four dimensions, and then selectively add the other dimensions to a subset of these moves in order to travel the correct distance in them too, without breaking validity of any move.

Proof.

Take start and end points x=(x1,…,xN),y=(y1,…,yN)\mathbf{x}=(x_1,\dots,x_N),\qquad \mathbf{y}=(y_1,\dots,y_N)x=(x1​,…,xN​),y=(y1​,…,yN​), and apply Proposition 1 to the first four coordinates. So for every extra coordinate rrr we choose one admissible triple and append εr,j\varepsilon_{r,j}εr,j​ to the jthj^{\text{th}}jth move. Propositions 1 and 2 prove the precise three-move claim for all N⩾4N \geqslant 4N⩾4, and therefore prove the theorem in these dimensions. The strong claim does not hold for the case N=3N = 3N=3, but fortunately we may find other sequences of at most three moves by brute force.

Proposition 3. On the N=3N = 3N=3 board, every pair of squares is joined by an SSS-bishop path of length at most three.

Proof.

At N=3N=3N=3, the entire board is small enough to work with directly: among the (83)2=262,144(8^3)^2 = 262{,}144(83)2=262,144 ordered pairs of squares, 19,71219{,}71219,712 lie at distance 111, 181,824181{,}824181,824 at distance 222, and 60,09660{,}09660,096 at distance 333.

Thus the theorem also holds for N=3N=3N=3. □\square□
