Electromagnetism Notes

Here are my revision notes for Electromagnetism, lectured in early 2024 by David Tong at the University of Cambridge.

Basics: Charge, Current, and Fields

The strength of the electromagnetic force experienced by a particle is proportional to its charge, measured in Coulombs. Here, we assume this charge takes values in \( \mathbb{R} \), but in reality this is quantised: charges must be integer multiples of the fundamental chargeThe electron has charge \(-e\). \(e \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \; \mathrm{C}\).

The charge density \( \rho(\mathbf{x}, t) \) is the charge per unit volume. The total charge in a region \(V\) is therefore \[ Q = \int_V \rho(\mathbf{x}, t) \text{d}V \] This might be expressed in terms of sheets or lines instead, as a 2D or 1D integral.

For any surface \( S \), \[ I = \int_S \mathbf{J} \cdot \text{d}S \] is the current, where \( \mathbf{J} \) is the current density.

If the charge \( \rho(\mathbf{x}, t) \) has velocity \( \mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x}, t) \), then the current density is fairly obviouslyignoring relativistic effects \( \rho \mathbf{v} \).

Charge is conserved. More specifically, it is conserved locallyGlobal conservation of charge only means the total charge in all of space is constant. We also require that a charge here can't disappear and instantly reappear on Jupiter, say.: it can only change due to continuous currents. This gives rise to the continuity equation: \[ \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0 \] Note that, by our definition of charge and the divergence theorem\( \int_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} \text{ d}V= \int_{\partial V} \mathbf{F} \cdot \text{d}\mathbf{S} \), the charge \( Q \) in some region \( V \) satisfies \[ \frac{\text{d} \rho}{\text{d} t} = \int_V \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} \text{d}V = - \int_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} \text{d}V = - \int_S \mathbf{J} \cdot \text{d}S \] If we take \( V = \mathbb{R}^3 \), the whole of space, then assuming there are no currents at infinity, \( Q' = 0 \) (global conservation of charge).

A field is a function from \( \mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^3 \text{ or } \mathbb{R} \). It assigns each point in time and space a vector or scalar quantity. We have the electric and magnetic fieldsThese are both vector fields: they assign each point a 3D vector showing the direction of the field. \( \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{x}, t) \) and \( \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{x}, t) \).

Fields cause particles to move according to the Lorentz Force Law: \[ \mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} ) \] We also have Maxwell's equationsNamed for James Clerk Maxwell, who published them in 1865.: \[ \begin{align*} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} &= \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \\ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} &= 0 \\ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} &= 0 \\ \nabla \times \mathbf{B} - \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} &= \mu_0 \mathbf{J} \end{align*} \] These use the constants \( \mu_0 \)Known as the magnetic constant, or permeability of free space. \( = 4 \pi \times 10^{-6} \; \mathrm{m} \; \mathrm{kg} \; \mathrm{C}^{-2} \) and \( \varepsilon_0 \)Known as the electric constant, or permittivity of free space. \( = 8.85 \pi \times 10^{-12} \; \mathrm{m}^{-3} \; \mathrm{kg}^{-1} \; \mathrm{s}^{-2} \; \mathrm{C}^2 \).

Electrostatics

Now, we ignore magnetic fieldsSet \( \mathbf{B} = 0 \) everywhere. and look for time-independent solutionsWhere the charge depends only on space, not time.. Our relevant equations are now \[ \begin{align*} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} &= \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \\ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= 0 \end{align*} \] as the other two disappear.

Consider a region \( V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3 \) with boundary \( S = \partial V \). Then we obtain Gauss' LawNamed, of course, for Carl Friedrich Gauss. \[ \int_S \mathbf{E} \cdot \text{d}\mathbf{S} = \int_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} \text{ d}V = \frac{1}{\varepsilon_0} \int_V \rho \text{ d}V = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}\] The flux of the electric field through \( S \) is defined as \[ \int_S \mathbf{E} \cdot \text{d}\mathbf{S}. \] Gauss's Law tells us that this depends only on the charge contained within the surface.

Consider a spherically symmetricDepending only on the distance from the origin, not the direction. charge density \( \rho(r) \) contained inside a ball of radius \(R\) such that \( r > R \implies \rho(r) = 0 \).

Then by symmetry, the force(and thus the electric field, which is parallel to the force in the absence of a magnetic field by the Lorentz Force Law) must point out radially: \(\mathbf{E} = E(r)\hat{\mathbf{r}} \). Thus it is conservative\( \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = 0 \).

For a sphere of radius \(r > R\) containing the charge, the total flux is thus \[ \int_S \mathbf{E} \cdot \text{ d}\mathbf{S} = E(r) \int_S \hat{\mathbf{r}} \cdot \text{d}\mathbf{S} = 4\pi r^2 E(r) = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0} \] Thus we have our expression for the electric field, and therefore Coulomb's lawNamed for Charles-Augustin de Coulomb, who described this law in 1785.: \[ \mathbf{E}(r) = \frac{Q}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r^2} \hat{\mathbf{r}} \implies \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{Qq}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{r}} \hat{\mathbf{r}} \] If there is some scalar field \( \phi \) such that \( \mathbf{E} = - \nabla \phi \), then we call \( \phi \) the electrostatic potential. This gives rise to the Poisson equationNamed for Sim√©on Denis Poisson. \[\nabla^2 \phi = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}\] When \(\rho = 0\), away from any charges, this is the Laplace equation.

Note that this is a linear equation: if we have two charges, we can simply add the corresponding potentials and fieldsThe ability to do this is known as the principle of superposition, and is unique to electromagnetism among the four fundamental forces.. To ensure \(\phi\) is unique, we insist that the the electrostatic potential vanishes at infinity.

Take a point particle with charge \(Q\) at the origin, so that \[\rho(\mathbf{r}) = Q \delta^3 (\mathbf{r})\] with \(\delta^3\) the three-dimensional Dirac delta functionThis "function" is zero everywhere except the origin, but the integral of it over any volume containing the origin is one.. We then must solve \[\nabla^2 \phi = - \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0} \delta^3 (\mathbf{r})\] Away from the origin, we have the Laplace equation, which is solved by \(\phi(\mathbf{r} = a r^{-1})\). Integrating over a sphere of radius \(r\) centred at the origin, \[ \int_V \nabla^2 \phi \text{ d}V = \int_S \nabla \phi \cdot \text{ d}\mathbf{S} = \int_S \frac{a}{r^2} \hat{\mathbf{r}} \cdot \text{ d}\mathbf{S} = -4 \pi a = - \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0} \implies a = \frac{Q}{4\pi \varepsilon_0} \implies \mathbf{E} = \frac{Q}{4\pi \varepsilon_0 r^2} \hat{\mathbf{r}}. \]

A field line is a line tangent to \(\mathbf{E}\). Field line density is proportional to \(|\mathbf{E}|\). Field lines begin only at charges or infinity, and can never intersect. Similarly, equipotentials are line of constant electrostatic potential \(\phi\). They are always perpendicular to the field lines. By convention, these lines point towards negative charges.

Recall that a particle of charge \(q\) has potential energy \(U = q \phi(\mathbf{r})\), which is the work it takes to bring the particle from infinity to its current location.

The energy of a particle system\( N \) charges \( q_i \) at positions \( \mathbf{r}_i \) is slightly different. \[ U = \frac{1}{8\pi \varepsilon_0} \sum_{i \neq j} \frac{q_i q_j}{|\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j|} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}{N} q_i \phi(\mathbf{r}_i). \] This is very similar to the continuous case: \[ U = \frac{1}{2} \int \rho (\mathbf{r}) \; \phi(\mathbf{r}) \; \mathrm{d}^3\mathbf{r} = \frac{\varepsilon_0}{2} \int (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) \phi \; \mathrm{d}^3\mathbf{r} = \frac{\varepsilon_0}{2} \int \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{E} \; \mathrm{d}^3\mathbf{r} \] This depends only on the field, not the charges.

A conductor is a region of space where many charges can move freely until they reach equilibrium. This necessitates \( \mathbf{E} = \mathbf{0} \) everywhere in the conductor. This then implies both that \( \phi \) is constantThis means that the surface is an equipotential, so the electric field must be perpendicular to it., and that \( \rho \) is zero everywhere inside the conductor: all net charge must be on the surface of the conductor.

Consider a spherical conductor with no net charge. Place a positive plate on the left and negative plate on the right to induce a rightward field. The field lines must be perpendicular to each surface, so they bend, and a surface charge is induced.

Magnetostatics

Now we look at the converse: steady-current situations\(\rho = 0 \implies \mathbf{E} = \mathbf{0}\) but \(\mathbf{J} \neq 0\): note that \(\rho = 0\) only implies the absence of net charge.. Maxwell's equations here are \[ \begin{align*} \nabla \times \mathbf{B} &= \mu_0 \mathbf{J} \\ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} &= 0 \end{align*} \] In this situation, as \(\rho = 0\), the net charge in and out of every point in space is null: for example in a wire, all electrons move at the same speed.

For a surface \(S\) with current \(\mathbf{J}\) flowing through it, we integrate \[ \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \mu_0 \int_S \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \mu_0 I \] which is Amp√®re's LawNamed for Andr√©-Marie Amp√®re, who also has the unit of current the amp named after him., where \(I\) is the current through the surface.

The general solution to \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\) is \(\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}\) for some vector potential \( \mathbf{A} \). Then \[ \nabla \times \mathbf{B} = -\nabla^2 \mathbf{A} + \nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}) = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} \] To make this easier to solve, we can do a gauge transformation. Any \(\mathbf{A}' = \mathbf{A} + \nabla \chi(\mathbf{x}) \) works, and we can choose this \(\chi\) so that \( \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}' = 0 \). Thus without loss of generality, we are solving \[ \nabla^2 \mathbf{A} = - \mu_0 \mathbf{J} \text{ where } \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0 \] We can solve this using Green's functionsWritten componentwise, we have that \( \nabla^2 A_i = -\mu_0 J_i \), which is the Poisson equation.: \[ \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|} \; dV' \] Now the magnetic field is the curl of this \(\mathbf{A}\), which gives us the Biot-Savart law: \[ \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \int \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times \frac{\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|^3} \; dV' \] or, if the current is on a closed curve, \[ \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi} \oint_C d\mathbf{r}' \times \frac{\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|^3} \; dV' \] For a circular loop \(C\) of radius \(R\) carrying current \(I\), we can use Biot-Savart: \[ \mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi} \oint_C \frac{d\mathbf{r}'}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|} \] We claim that far from the loop, this approximates a magnetic dipoleAn electric dipole is a separate positive and negative point charge. A magnetic dipole is the magnetic equivalent of this.. We use the Taylor expansion of the denominator: \[ \mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi} \oint_C \left( \frac{1}{r} + \frac{\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{r}'}{r^3} \cdots \right) d\mathbf{r}' \] The first term is a constant and so vanishes on a closed loop. We take only the second term in the limit. For any constant vector \(\mathbf{g}\), the integral of \(\mathbf{g} \cdot \mathbf{r}\) along a closed curve \(C\) is \(\mathbf{S} \times \mathbf{g}\), where \(\mathbf{S}\) is the vector areaDefined as \( \int_S \text{d}\mathbf{S} \) of the surface \(S\) bounded by \(C\). Thus \[ \mathbf{A} \approx \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi} \frac{I \mathbf{S} \times \mathbf{r}}{r^3}. \]
